Given any real matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$, the singular value decomposition
gives a factorization

$$
A = U\,\Sigma\,V^{\top}
$$

where $U \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ and $V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ are orthogonal,
and $\Sigma$ is diagonal with non-negative entries $\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge \cdots \ge 0$.

The $\sigma_i$ are the **singular values** — the principal axes along which $A$ stretches
the unit ball. They quantify how much signal each orthogonal direction carries.

## Low-rank approximation

Truncating to the top $k$ components produces the optimal rank-$k$ approximation in
the Frobenius norm (Eckart–Young):

$$
A_k \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_i\, u_i\, v_i^{\top}, \qquad
\min_{\mathrm{rank}(B) \le k} \|A - B\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i > k} \sigma_i^{2}}.
$$

This is the reason SVD underlies image compression, PCA, latent semantic analysis,
and the low-rank structure assumed by modern recommender systems.

## A geometric reading

Every linear map factors as **rotate → scale → rotate**. That is the whole story:

- $V^\top$ picks a principal coordinate frame in the domain,
- $\Sigma$ stretches along those axes by the singular values,
- $U$ places the result into an output frame.

Numerically, SVD is also the stablest way to compute pseudo-inverses, condition numbers,
and rank — all from the same $\Sigma$.
