Shannon defined the entropy of a discrete random variable $X$ taking values $x_i$
with probabilities $P(x_i)$ as

$$
H(X) = -\sum_{i} P(x_i)\, \log P(x_i).
$$

It is the **average number of bits** required to describe a draw from $X$, and it
is simultaneously the lower bound on lossless compression.

## Channel capacity

For a noisy channel, the capacity is the maximum mutual information between the
input $X$ and output $Y$:

$$
C = \max_{P(X)}\; I(X;Y) = \max_{P(X)}\; \bigl( H(Y) - H(Y \mid X) \bigr).
$$

Shannon's noisy-channel coding theorem states: any rate $R < C$ can be achieved with
arbitrarily small error in the limit of long block codes; any rate $R > C$ cannot.

## Why this still matters

Every bit of useful work a system does — every transmission, every inference, every
compression — runs into one of Shannon's walls. Modern LLMs are, in a very literal
sense, learned approximations of $-\log P(x)$: they bet on the next token by estimating
how much surprise it carries, and their efficiency is bounded by exactly this quantity.
