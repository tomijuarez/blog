> The state of a quantum system is represented by a unit vector in a complex Hilbert space,
> yet the environment continuously erodes this purity.

A pure state $|\psi\rangle$ evolves under the Schrödinger equation:

$$
i\hbar\,\partial_t\,|\Psi(t)\rangle = \hat{H}\,|\Psi(t)\rangle
$$

When coupled to an environment, however, the effective dynamics are described by a
density matrix $\rho$ whose evolution follows the **Lindblad master equation**:

$$
\dot{\rho} = -\frac{i}{\hbar}[\hat{H}, \rho] + \sum_k \left( L_k \rho L_k^\dagger - \tfrac{1}{2}\{L_k^\dagger L_k, \rho\} \right)
$$

Decoherence erodes off-diagonal amplitudes. In the eigenbasis of the pointer observable,
the density matrix decays toward a diagonal form:

$$
\rho_{ij}(t) \sim \rho_{ij}(0)\, e^{-\gamma_{ij} t}, \qquad i \ne j
$$

## Why this matters for error correction

Stabilizer codes only work when the noise is sufficiently Markovian and local. The
structure of the Lindblad operators $L_k$ determines which error channels a given
code can correct. For a single-qubit depolarizing channel, the effective fidelity is
bounded by

$$
F(\rho,\sigma) = \left(\mathrm{Tr}\sqrt{\sqrt{\rho}\,\sigma\,\sqrt{\rho}}\right)^{2}.
$$

Inline math also works cleanly: the commutator $[\hat{A}, \hat{B}] = \hat{A}\hat{B} - \hat{B}\hat{A}$
captures the non-commutativity that gives quantum mechanics its texture.
